SECTION 1 — PROBLEM & MOTIVATION
======================================================================

HEADER: Problem & Motivation

BODY:
The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) asks: given n locations, find the
shortest Hamiltonian cycle visiting each exactly once. Despite its simple
formulation, TSP is NP-hard — no polynomial exact algorithm exists unless
P=NP.

TSP drives modern logistics (Meituan, Uber Eats, Amazon last-mile delivery)
and extends to PCB drilling, VLSI chip design, and genome sequencing.

TSP is algorithmically unique because it:
  • Is NP-hard, yet admits a 1.5× approximation (Christofides, 1976)
  • Is simple enough for deep learning to learn useful heuristics
  • Is complex enough that pure ML fails without classical components
  • Is the canonical benchmark where new algorithmic ideas are validated

After decades of research, a 2017 classical heuristic (LKH3) still dominates
the speed-quality frontier at every scale we tested. Meanwhile, a new wave
of neural solvers — powered by diffusion models and reinforcement learning —
has emerged since 2023. This project asks:

  Can we do better by COMBINING classical and neural approaches,
  rather than choosing between them?

SECTION 2 — ALGORITHM LANDSCAPE
======================================================================

HEADER: Algorithm Landscape: Six Methods, Four Paradigms

BODY:
We study six methods spanning the full spectrum — from simple greedy
construction to cutting-edge neural generation. The four classical
algorithms were implemented from scratch in Python/NumPy. The two
neural methods were reproduced from their published codebases with
compatibility patches for modern PyTorch/CUDA/Windows.

Classical Methods (implemented from scratch in Python/NumPy):

  • Nearest Neighbor — Greedy baseline: repeatedly visits closest unvisited
    node (O(n²), 20–30% gap).

  • Christofides (1976) — The only polynomial algorithm with a provable 1.5×
    guarantee for Metric TSP: MST → Blossom MWPM → Eulerian shortcut (O(n³)).

  • C+2opt — Christofides followed by 2-opt local search that eliminates
    crossing edge pairs; our NumPy-vectorized 2-opt achieves ~10× speedup
    (~4.3% mean TSPLIB gap, strongest classical baseline).

  • LKH3 (Helsgaun, 2017) — Gold standard: generalizes k-opt moves (k=2–5)
    guided by α-nearness from minimum 1-trees (~0.4% gap, the ceiling).

Neural Methods (reproduced from published codebases):

  • DIFUSCO (NeurIPS 2023) — Categorical denoising diffusion on adjacency
    matrices: a 12-layer GNN (5.3M params) generates an edge heatmap over
    50 DDIM steps → greedy merge → 2-opt polish.

  • DualOpt (AAAI 2025) — Divide-and-optimize: grid partition + LKH3 sub-
    solves → 3 cascaded RL-trained neural revisers (710K params) refine
    the tour; constant ~6s inference regardless of instance size n.

SECTION 3 — DIFUSCO → DUALOPT HYBRID PIPELINE
======================================================================

HEADER: DIFUSCO → DualOpt: A Hybrid Neural Pipeline for TSP

This is the core contribution of this project. Rather than treating
DIFUSCO and DualOpt as competing methods, we combined them — taking the
generative power of diffusion from DIFUSCO and the learned local search
from DualOpt. The result: a pipeline that outperforms both.

CORE INSIGHT (from the figure):
  "A Hybrid pipeline: DIFUSCO provides global structure,
   DualOpt provides local precision."

  • DIFUSCO's diffusion process captures GLOBAL tour structure —
    the GNN sees the entire n×n adjacency matrix simultaneously.
    But its raw output has local defects (self-crossings,
    suboptimal turns).

  • DualOpt's revisers excel at LOCAL refinement — trained via
    REINFORCE to directly minimize tour length within sliding
    windows. But they need a reasonable starting tour.

  The hybrid pipeline lets each component do what it does best.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
PIPELINE ARCHITECTURE (refer to Gemini_Generated_Image_...png)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The Gemini figure shows the complete two-phase pipeline with exact
labels. The text below explains each component's origin and role.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PHASE 1: DIFFUSCO CONSTRUCTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Random Noise (random Bernoulli edges)
  → DIFUSCO Model (Diffusion Model): 12-layer GNN, ~5.3M parameters
     [Taken from DIFUSCO: the Anisotropic Gated GNN from Sun & Yang.
      Trained 20 epochs on TSP-50 with Christofides+2opt labels.
      Supervised cross-entropy loss on edge classification.]
  → 50 Denoising Steps (Cosine schedule, DDIM)
     [Taken from DIFUSCO: categorical diffusion process, cosine noise
      schedule. The GNN iteratively predicts clean edges from noisy
      adjacency, conditioned on coordinates and timestep embedding.]
  → Edge Probability Heatmap H[i][j] ∈ [0,1]
     [H[i][j] = confidence that edge (i,j) belongs to a good tour.
      This encodes the model's GLOBAL understanding of edge quality.]
  → Greedy Tour Extraction (Merge edges by rank H[i][j] / distance)
     [Taken from DIFUSCO: rank all n(n−1)/2 candidate edges by
      heatmap-score-to-distance ratio. Insert into partial tour
      while preserving degree ≤ 2 and preventing sub-cycles.]
  → Initial DIFUSCO-Generated Tour π₀
     [Structurally coherent skeleton; typical gap: ~13% vs optimal.]

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PHASE 2: NEURAL REFINEMENT
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Neural Reviser Models:
     [Taken from DualOpt: attention-based policy networks trained
      via REINFORCE with rollout baseline, ~710K parameters total.
      Three pretrained checkpoints at k=50,20,10.]

  Sliding window mechanism:
     [Taken from DualOpt: decompose tour into overlapping windows
      of k consecutive nodes. Each reviser processes windows
      sequentially, proposing improved sub-tour orderings.]

  ✗ No Heatmap-Guided Skip (Failed mechanism)
     [WHAT WE REMOVED: the pipeline originally used DIFUSCO's heatmap
      to skip revision on confident windows. This had zero effect —
      the reviser is already near-optimal on TSP-50, and the heatmap
      is too poorly calibrated to identify which windows need revision.
      See Section 5 for analysis.]

  → k=50 Coarse Reviser (25 iter.): Fixes global structure
  → k=20 Medium Reviser (10 iter.): Fixes regional issues
  → k=10 Fine Reviser (5 iter.): Polishes local details

  Initial Tour π₀ → [3 revisers] → Final Optimized Delivery Tour π*

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FUSION ENGINEERING — What We Built to Make This Work
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  The DIFUSCO and DualOpt codebases were NOT designed to interoperate.
  We resolved three integration challenges:

  1. Package name conflict: Both use a top-level "utils" package.
     → Path isolation: dynamically switch sys.path so each module
       imports from its own codebase.

  2. Data format mismatch: DIFUSCO outputs a permutation list π;
     DualOpt expects a "seeds" tensor of shape (1, n, 2).
     → Explicit conversion layer between the two formats.

  3. Checkpoint incompatibility: DIFUSCO uses PyTorch Lightning .ckpt;
     DualOpt uses raw torch.save state_dicts.
     → Unified loader that bridges both formats transparently.

  New module: DualOpt-improved/utils/difusco_pipeline.py (~160 lines)

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHY IT WORKS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  DIFUSCO's GNN processes the ENTIRE n×n adjacency matrix jointly —
  capturing long-range dependencies for globally coherent tour
  skeletons. DualOpt's revisers operate within LOCAL windows —
  producing precise corrections at the neighborhood level.

  Neither is sufficient alone:
  • DIFUSCO alone: global coherence without local precision → 13% gap
  • DualOpt alone: depends on initial tour quality; cannot fix
    fundamentally broken global structure when n > 100
  • Together: DIFUSCO is the architect drawing the blueprint;
    DualOpt's revisers are the craftsmen refining each room.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
KEY RESULTS (detailed in Section 4)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  DIFUSCO raw (no refinement):    6.696  (+13.67% vs GT)
  DIFUSCO + 2-opt (standard):     5.969  (+1.32%  vs GT)
  DIFUSCO → DualOpt (OURS):     5.793  (−1.67%  vs GT)  ← BEST

  The pipeline extracts 24% more improvement than 2-opt alone.
  On TSPLIB kroA100, it surpasses original DualOpt (+0.9 pp).

SECTION 4 — EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
======================================================================

HEADER: Experimental Results

SETUP. We evaluate on three datasets: (1) Random Uniform TSP — 10 held-out
instances at each of n = 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000, matching DIFUSCO's training
distribution; (2) TSPLIB Standard Benchmark — 8 symmetric instances (51 to
1002 nodes) with known optimal values, the authoritative test of generalization;
(3) Clustered Delivery TSP — 4 Gaussian clusters + scattered points at
n = 50, 100, 200, representing real-world delivery density patterns. All
methods evaluated on identical instances. Ground truth for TSP-50: Christofides
+ 2-opt with 5,000 iterations. TSPLIB ground truth: known optimal values from
the literature. LKH3 serves as the speed-quality ceiling.

We report results in five tables (compiled separately as poster_tables.pdf).
Refer to the table numbers below for exact data; the text summarizes key findings.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
A. TSP-50 BENCHMARK — All Methods on Equal Ground  [Tables 1, 4, 7]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 1 compares all 6 methods on 10 identical random TSP-50 instances.
DIFUSCO+2opt matches the C+2opt baseline (0.3% gap) after only 20 epochs
of training — effective learning from suboptimal labels. DualOpt surpasses
all methods (−2.1% vs GT), finding improvements that 5,000-iteration 2-opt
missed, because its REINFORCE-trained revisers directly optimize tour length.

Table 4 shows our hybrid pipeline's improvement chain: raw DIFUSCO starts
at +13.67% gap; adding 2-opt closes it to +1.32%; replacing 2-opt with
DualOpt's revisers achieves −1.67% — the BEST result of any method and the
only one to beat the training labels. The reviser extracts 24% more
improvement than 2-opt from the same heatmap.

Table 7 summarizes all 5 improvement strategies attempted. Only the pipeline
(#2) succeeded; the other four, which all tried to guide the reviser's
internal search using DIFUSCO's heatmap confidence, failed because TSP-50
is essentially "solved" for DualOpt's reviser.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
B. TSPLIB GENERALIZATION — Cross-Scale and Cross-Distribution  [Table 2]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 2 reports gaps vs known optimal on 8 TSPLIB instances (51–1002 nodes).
DIFUSCO, trained only on random uniform TSP-50, generalizes to TSP-1002
(20× larger) with just 7.1% gap — the GNN learns size-agnostic edge quality.
DualOpt achieves near-perfect results within its training window (berlin52:
0.03% gap) but degrades catastrophically beyond n≈100 (ch150: 45.3%, worse
than Nearest Neighbor) due to fixed reviser window sizes. Christofides+2opt
remains a strong baseline: 4.3% mean gap with no learned components.

Key pattern: DIFUSCO and DualOpt show complementary generalization —
DIFUSCO transfers robustly across sizes but is bounded by label quality;
DualOpt is precise within its window but brittle beyond. This complementarity
motivates our hybrid pipeline.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
C. PIPELINE VALIDATION  [Tables 4, 5]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 5 validates the DIFUSCO→DualOpt pipeline on 4 TSPLIB instances (n ≤ 100).
On kroA100, our pipeline achieves 1.81% gap — surpassing the original DualOpt
(2.71%) by 0.9 pp. This is notable because DIFUSCO was trained on random-uniform
data, yet the pipeline transfers to TSPLIB with competitive results. On smaller
instances (eil51, berlin52, eil76), the original DualOpt retains a modest edge,
as its LKH grid-solve initialization provides a stronger starting point than
diffusion-based construction at these scales.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
D. SCALABILITY — From 100 to 1000 Nodes  [Table 9]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 9 shows solution quality, gap vs LKH3, and runtime for all classic
methods across n = 100, 200, 500, 1000. C+2opt maintains a steady 2.7–3.6%
gap vs LKH3 independent of instance size — impressive for a pure heuristic.
However, the Christofides cubic bottleneck dominates beyond n≈200: at n=1000,
C+2opt takes 51.2s vs LKH3's 1.2s (43× faster), while LKH3 also produces
better solutions. LKH3 dominates the speed-quality Pareto frontier at every
scale we tested.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
E. LKH3 — THE GOLD STANDARD CEILING  [Table 10]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 10 reports LKH3 vs known optimal on all 8 TSPLIB instances. LKH3 achieves
a mean gap of 0.40%, with four instances within 0.05% of optimal. This places
a clear ceiling on all methods: even the best neural solver (DualOpt, 2.2% mean
for n≤100) remains ~5× further from optimal than LKH3. On tsp225, LKH3 found
a tour better than the commonly cited optimum. The key takeaway: neural methods
have not yet closed the quality-speed Pareto frontier — but they offer different
tradeoffs (generalization, parallelizability, and the ability to learn from data
rather than hand-crafted rules).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
F. ABLATION — What Makes DIFUSCO Work?  [Table 3]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Table 3 ablates DIFUSCO's inference pipeline: {10,20,50} denoising steps ×
{no 2-opt, +2-opt}. Raw diffusion output (any #steps) has ~13% gap — WORSE
than pure Christofides (9.0%). Adding 2-opt closes the gap to ~0.3%. Only
10 denoising steps are needed: 50 steps gives marginal extra gain at 4× the
inference cost. The diffusion model learns "which edges are plausible," not
"how to build tight tours" — 2-opt bridges the gap.

SECTION 5 — FIVE IMPROVEMENTS ATTEMPTED
======================================================================

HEADER: Five Improvement Strategies, One Successful Pipeline

We designed and tested 5 strategies to integrate DIFUSCO's edge
probability heatmap with DualOpt's learned local search. Four failed.
One succeeded — and became the hybrid pipeline described in Section 3.

THIS SECTION IS BUILT AROUND THE FIGURE (OCR text from the image):

The figure shows the DIFUSCO→DualOpt pipeline architecture, annotated
with WHERE each improvement strategy intervenes and HOW it performed.
The text below explains each annotation point on the figure.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
THE FIGURE — Annotated Pipeline with Five Intervention Points
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The figure follows the same two-phase structure as Section 3, but each
improvement strategy is shown as an ANNOTATION at its intervention
point along the pipeline flow:

  Random Noise → DIFUSCO Model (12-layer GNN, ~5.3M params)
    → 50 Denoising Steps (Cosine schedule, DDIM)
    → Edge Probability Heatmap
    → Greedy Tour Extraction (Merge edges by rank H[i][j]/dist)
    → Initial DIFUSCO-Generated Tour
    → [IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES INTERVENE HERE]
    → k=50 Coarse Reviser (25 iter.): Fixes global structure
    → k=20 Medium Reviser (10 iter.): Fixes regional issues
    → k=10 Fine Reviser (5 iter.): Polishes local details
    → Final Optimized Delivery Tour

The five strategies are shown at their respective intervention points
with color-coded outcome markers (success / no effect / degradation).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRATEGY #2 — DIFUSCO → DualOpt Pipeline  [THE SUCCESS]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  INTERVENTION POINT (on figure): BEFORE the DualOpt revisers.
  The pipeline itself IS Strategy #2 — replace DualOpt's heuristic
  initial tour (LKH grid-solve) with a DIFUSCO-generated tour built
  entirely from random noise via diffusion.

  WHY IT'S ON THE FIGURE: The pipeline flow from "Random Noise" through
  "Initial DIFUSCO-Generated Tour" into the three revisers IS the
  architecture of Strategy #2. This is the SUCCESSFUL strategy:
  change the INPUT to the reviser rather than its internal process.

  RESULT: −1.67% vs GT on TSP-50 (BEST of all methods). The DualOpt
  reviser extracts 24% more improvement than 2-opt from the same
  heatmap. On TSPLIB kroA100: 1.81% gap, surpassing original DualOpt.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRATEGY #1 — Heatmap-Guided Reviser  [NO EFFECT — Δ = 0.00%]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  INTERVENTION POINT (on figure): Between the Edge Probability Heatmap
  output and the k=50 Coarse Reviser — marked as ✗ "No Heatmap-Guided
  Skip (Failed mechanism)".

  STRATEGY: During each reviser pass, compute the average DIFUSCO
  heatmap confidence for edges in each sliding window. If confidence
  exceeds a threshold (τ = 0.5), SKIP the reviser for that window —
  the edges are presumed already good.

  WHY IT FAILED: On TSP-50 with k=50, there is only ONE window (the
  entire tour) — nothing can be skipped. With k=20, only 3 windows
  exist; skipping any means abandoning ~40% of edges. Additionally,
  the reviser decodes greedily (deterministic): skipped windows would
  have converged to the same result anyway.

  ANNOTATION ON FIGURE: The ✗ marker indicates this mechanism was
  REMOVED from the final pipeline — it added complexity with zero
  measurable benefit.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRATEGY #3 — Adaptive Window Sizing  [NO IMPROVEMENT]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  INTERVENTION POINT (on figure): At the transition between the three
  reviser stages (k=50 → k=20 → k=10) — dynamically adjusts the
  number of iterations per window based on a diagnostic signal.

  STRATEGY: Run a lightweight 2-opt diagnostic (10 iterations) on the
  current tour to identify "unstable" edges (those 2-opt changes).
  Allocate MORE reviser iterations to windows with high instability
  density, FEWER to already-stable windows.

  WHY IT FAILED: 2-opt converges to the SAME local optimum the reviser
  already reaches. The "unstable" edges 2-opt identifies are exactly
  the ones the reviser would have fixed anyway. The diagnostic provides
  no information the reviser doesn't already have. Equal iteration
  allocation was already optimal.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRATEGY #4 — Fragment Freezing  [MIXED — 4/10 improved, unstable]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  INTERVENTION POINT (on figure): BEFORE the reviser cascade — locks
  a subset of edges in place before revision begins.

  STRATEGY: Run DIFUSCO AND Christofides+2opt independently on the
  same instance. Extract edge sets from both tours. Find the edge
  INTERSECTION — edges that BOTH methods independently select. Freeze
  these agreed-upon edges (mark as immutable). Run DualOpt reviser
  constrained to only rearrange non-frozen nodes. The rationale: if two
  fundamentally different solvers independently agree on an edge, it is
  almost certainly optimal.

  RESULT: 4/10 instances improved, with gains up to −5.16% (the single
  best per-instance gain of ANY method). However, aggregate performance
  DEGRADED (+1.51% mean vs original DualOpt). The agreement rate between
  DIFUSCO and C+2opt is only 61.6% — they disagree on ~40% of edges.
  When both solvers agree on a WRONG edge (~40% of agreement), the error
  is permanently locked in and the reviser cannot fix it (worst case:
  +15.43% on instance 9). On TSPLIB: complete failure (invalid tours
  below OPT — physically impossible for metric TSP).

  SCIENTIFIC VALUE: This is the ONLY method besides #2 that shows positive
  per-instance results, confirming that structural consensus between
  independent solvers is a stronger signal than learned probability scores.
  But the method is unstable — agreement can encode errors. The insight:
  consensus-based freezing is promising but requires a third "tiebreaker"
  mechanism for disputed edges. See Table 11 for per-instance data.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STRATEGY #5 — Destroy-and-Repair  [DEGRADATION]
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  INTERVENTION POINT (on figure): AFTER the Initial DIFUSCO-Generated
  Tour, BEFORE the reviser cascade — actively destroys and rebuilds
  portions of the initial tour.

  STRATEGY: Use DIFUSCO's heatmap to identify the K edges with LOWEST
  confidence. DESTROY these edges — breaking the tour into K+1
  disconnected segments. REPAIR by reconnecting segments via nearest-
  neighbor greedy insertion + 2-opt polish. A Hyper-Repair variant
  compresses intact segments into hyper-edges and uses the neural
  reviser as the repair operator (inspired by DRHG, AAAI 2025).

  WHY IT FAILED: Random destruction + greedy repair produces WORSE
  tours than simply running the reviser on the intact initial tour.
  The reviser's learned optimization is fundamentally more effective
  than greedy heuristics at fixing the same edges the heatmap flags.
  Destroying edges removes information the reviser could have used.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
THE FUNDAMENTAL INSIGHT (bottom annotation on the figure)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  "A Hybrid pipeline: DIFUSCO provides global structure,
   DualOpt provides local precision."

  The four failed strategies (#1,3,4,5) all share a common flaw:
  they attempt to GUIDE the reviser's internal search process using
  DIFUSCO's heatmap confidence scores. This fails because:

  (a) The reviser is already SATURATED on TSP-50 — it reaches the
      same local optimum regardless of perturbation.
  (b) The DIFUSCO heatmap is POORLY CALIBRATED — only ~45% of high-
      confidence edges are actually optimal, even in-distribution.

  The successful strategy (#2) works because it changes the INITIAL
  CONDITIONS — the starting tour — rather than trying to steer the
  optimizer mid-flight. The diffusion model generates a structurally
  novel starting point that the reviser has never seen during RL
  training, and the reviser's learned policy generalizes to it.

  Lesson for hybrid system design: when a learned optimizer has
  saturated, invest in improving the GENERATED INPUT, not in
  constraining the optimizer's search.

SECTION 6 — KEY INSIGHTS & CONCLUSIONS
======================================================================

HEADER: Summary & Key Insights

WHAT WE DID. Reproduced DIFUSCO (diffusion) and DualOpt (RL divide-and-conquer)
on a single RTX 2060, implemented four classical algorithms from scratch, and
built a hybrid pipeline — DIFUSCO generates the initial tour, DualOpt's revisers
refine it. Tested four other heatmap-guided strategies; all revealed fundamental
limits of learned optimizers.

WHEN THE PIPELINE WORKS. TSP-50: −1.67\% vs GT, best of all methods. kroA100
(TSPLIB): 1.81\%, surpasses original DualOpt (2.71\%). Requires per-scale DIFUSCO
training — the diffusion GNN must produce a meaningful heatmap at the target size.

WHEN IT DOES NOT. Cross-size without retraining: TSP-50 model on TSP-100
degrades (−2.24\%). Small TSPLIB (eil51/berlin52): LKH grid-solve initialization
beats diffusion at n≈50. TSPLIB out-of-distribution: heatmap gap 36–60\%,
all heatmap strategies collapse. Beyond TSP-100: dense GNN exceeds 6\,GB VRAM.

KEY TAKEAWAYS:

1. HYBRID $>$ EITHER ALONE. DIFUSCO (global) $+$ DualOpt (local) $=$ $-$1.67\% vs GT.

2. CHANGE THE INPUT, NOT THE PROCESS. Replacing the initial tour (\#2) succeeded;
   guiding the reviser internally (\#1,3,4,5) failed — it already saturates.

3. CLASSICAL WINS AT SMALL SCALES. At $n\le1000$, LKH3 (0.4\% gap, 1.2s) and
   C$+$2opt (4.3\%) are fast, near-optimal, and need no training.

4. NEURAL METHODS TARGET LARGE SCALES. DualOpt beats LKH3 by 1.40\% on TSP-100K
   (104$\times$ faster); DIFUSCO scales to TSP-10K. Our ceiling was $n=1000$ on a
   single RTX 2060 — the hybrid pipeline should be even more impactful at scale.

5. 2-OPT DRIVES DIFUSCO. Raw diffusion: $\sim$13\% gap $\rightarrow$ $+$2-opt:
   $\sim$0.3\%. 10 steps suffice. GNN generalizes across sizes (TSP-50 $\rightarrow$
   TSP-1002: 7.1\% gap).

6. FIVE IMPROVEMENTS CHART THE DESIGN SPACE. \#1: confidence $\neq$ guidance. \#2:
   hybrid pipelines work. \#3: weak optimizers can't guide strong ones. \#4:
   solver consensus shows promise (4/10 improved, up to $-$5.16\%) but needs a
   tiebreaker. \#5: once saturated, perturbations only degrade.
