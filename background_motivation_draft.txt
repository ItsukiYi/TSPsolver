VERSION A — "The Gap" Framing (tension-driven)
======================================================================

TITLE: The Traveling Salesman Problem
SUBTITLE: Classical algorithms still lead. Can neural methods catch up?

BODY:
The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) — find the shortest Hamiltonian cycle
visiting n locations — is NP-hard and ubiquitous in modern logistics: every
Meituan delivery route, every Amazon warehouse tour is a TSP instance.

Classical algorithms dominate practice: LKH3 (2017) solves TSP-1000 in 1.2
seconds with 0.7% optimality gap, while neural methods require 800× more
time for worse results. Yet neural approaches offer something classical
solvers cannot: the ability to learn from data, generalize across instance
distributions, and scale via GPU parallelism.

This project asks: can we close the gap — not by replacing classical
algorithms, but by combining them with neural methods?

STRENGTHS:
  • Punchy, creates curiosity
  • Highlights the tension between classical and neural
  • Frames the hybrid approach as the natural resolution
WEAKNESSES:
  • Slightly dismissive of neural methods' achievements
  • "LKH3 still wins" might overshadow the project's contributions

VERSION B — "The Complement" Framing (insight-driven)
======================================================================

TITLE: Delivery Route Optimization
SUBTITLE: When classical algorithms and neural networks work together,
         both win.

BODY:
TSP is a cornerstone of combinatorial optimization: NP-hard, yet practically
crucial — from last-mile delivery to chip design. After decades of research,
classical solvers like LKH3 achieve near-optimal results at remarkable speed.
Meanwhile, deep learning has produced a new generation of TSP solvers based
on diffusion models and reinforcement learning.

We reproduced two leading neural methods — DIFUSCO (NeurIPS 2023) and
DualOpt (AAAI 2025) — alongside four classical algorithms, and discovered
that their strengths are complementary: DIFUSCO captures global tour
structure via diffusion; DualOpt's revisers excel at local refinement.
Neither is sufficient alone. Combined, they achieve results neither can
reach independently.

STRENGTHS:
  • Positive framing — "both win" rather than "one beats the other"
  • Sets up the pipeline contribution naturally
  • Accurate to what the project actually found
WEAKNESSES:
  • Less dramatic hook
  • Requires the reader to already know what DIFUSCO/DualOpt are

VERSION C — "The Journey" Framing (narrative-driven)
======================================================================

TITLE: From Christofides to Diffusion: Rethinking TSP
SUBTITLE: What happens when classical algorithms meet modern neural solvers —
         and why combining them is better than choosing sides.

BODY:
For 50 years, the Traveling Salesman Problem has been the proving ground
for algorithmic ideas — from Christofides' elegant 1.5× approximation (1976)
to the fiercely optimized LKH3 heuristic (2017). In 2023, diffusion models
entered the arena: DIFUSCO showed that Graph Neural Networks could generate
TSP tours by iteratively denoising random adjacency matrices.

We set out to answer a simple question: are these neural methods actually
better than classical algorithms? The answer turned out to be more
interesting than "yes" or "no." Neural methods have complementary strengths
that classical solvers lack — and vice versa. Our key contribution is a
hybrid pipeline that combines DIFUSCO's global generative capability with
DualOpt's learned local search, achieving better results than either method
alone.

STRENGTHS:
  • Historical sweep gives intellectual weight
  • "More interesting than yes/no" is a genuine hook
  • Naturally leads to the hybrid pipeline as the resolution
WEAKNESSES:
  • Longest version (~130 words) — may need trimming for poster
  • Might be too narrative-heavy for a poster (posters favor bullet points)

======================================================================
VERSION D — "The Question" Framing (minimal, punchy)
======================================================================

TITLE: TSP: Classical vs Neural — or Classical + Neural?
SUBTITLE: We reproduced, compared, and combined two SOTA neural solvers
         with four classical algorithms. The hybrid wins.

BODY:
TSP is NP-hard. It's also everywhere — delivery routing, circuit design,
genome sequencing. Classical algorithms (Christofides, LKH3) give provable
guarantees or near-optimal results. Neural methods (diffusion models,
reinforcement learning) promise to learn better heuristics from data.

We reproduced both worlds on consumer hardware, benchmarked them on the
same instances, and asked: do they compete, or do they complement?

Answer: they complement. Our DIFUSCO → DualOpt hybrid pipeline — diffusion
for global structure, learned local search for precision — outperforms all
individual methods, achieving −1.67% vs ground truth on TSP-50.

STRENGTHS:
  • Shortest and punchiest (~90 words)
  • Title is the thesis statement
  • Immediately states the key result
WEAKNESSES:
  • Less background context for non-TSP-savvy readers
  • Skips the nuance of why classical+neural works

======================================================================
RECOMMENDATION
======================================================================

For a CS240 poster, I recommend VERSION B (The Complement Framing) with
elements from Version D's conciseness. Rationale:

1. CS240's theme is "how classical algorithms solve modern problems" —
   Version B directly addresses this by showing classical ≠ obsolete.

2. The "complementary strengths" insight is the project's most original
   intellectual contribution — the poster should lead with it.

3. It avoids the trap of making the poster about "neural vs classical"
   (a horse race) and instead makes it about "how to integrate both"
   (an architectural question).

4. For a poster, the reader should understand the POINT in 10 seconds.
   Version B's subtitle does that: "When classical algorithms and neural
   networks work together, both win."

optimization is not classical OR neural — it is classical AND neural.
